Nutrition is the pillar most colonised by ideology. Each camp — low-carb, plant-based, carnivore, fasting — arrives with the fervour of a faith and the marketing budget of a startup. The volume is inversely proportional to the certainty the science actually affords.

I want to offer something quieter: a handful of principles that survive every fad, framed for people who would rather eat well for thirty years than perfectly for thirty days.

## Start with what is not in dispute

Beneath the noise, there is striking consensus. Nearly every credible approach agrees on more than its proponents admit:

- **Eat mostly whole foods.** The single highest-leverage change is reducing ultra-processed food. This matters more than the macronutrient debate that follows it.
- **Prioritise protein.** Most people, and especially most people over fifty, eat too little. Protein protects the muscle that protects your healthspan.
- **Make plants the volume.** Fibre, polyphenols, and micronutrients arrive together, in a package no supplement reproduces.
- **Respect the glucose response.** Stable energy and metabolic health follow from how, not only what, you eat.

Notice that none of this requires choosing a tribe.

> The argument is rarely about food. It is about identity. Decline the membership and the choices get simpler.

## The thing the diets get right — and wrong

Most popular diets work, briefly, for the same unremarkable reason: they remove a category of low-quality food and impose attention where there was none. The restriction is incidental; the attention is the active ingredient.

They fail for an equally unremarkable reason: they are not livable. A protocol you abandon in March is worth less than a modest pattern you keep for life. Adherence is the variable that dwarfs all others, and adherence is personal. The best diet is, genuinely and unglamorously, the good-enough one you will actually sustain.

## A considered default

For most of the people I advise, a durable pattern looks like this: protein at every meal, plants in volume, whole-food carbohydrates earned through movement, and a relationship with food that is attentive without being anxious. Around that, there is enormous room for personal taste, culture, and pleasure — which is precisely the point. Eating well should make a good life better, not narrower.

Hold the principles firmly and the rules loosely. The dogma will change with the season. The fundamentals have not moved in a very long time.
